Justice Takes a Vacation

With Elizabeth Fox-Solomon leaving Western New York for Atlanta and the ACLU, local activists and legal officials are scrambling to fill the void. Here's why it may be impossible.

By KEVIN PURDY
Displaced News Staff Reporter
5/4/2004
  Click to view larger picture
Elizabeth Fox-Solomon, right, with an unknown friend at UB's 2004 graduation ceremony.

  Click to view larger picture
Former UB President William R. Greiner: "If
we can't find something to keep people like Elizabeth here in Buffalo, then we're doing something wrong."

When members of the Political Science department at the University at Buffalo met last spring to determine the recipient of the department's highest honor for 2003, the meeting lasted only five minutes.

Charles Lamb, a political science professor at UB, said the committee spent about five minutes considering other candidates, and then 30 minutes trading stories about one student: Elizabeth Fox-Solomon.

"Were there other exemplary students that year? Of course so," said Lamb. "But when it comes down to who we pin our hopes on for academic greatness, Elizabeth was far and away the only choice."

But Fox-Solomon, like hundreds of UB graduates and other young adults, will soon leave Western New York for warmer climates and better jobs.

She'll head to Atlanta this summer for an internship with the American Civil Liberties Union, and is reportedly looking to transfer from UB's law school to one near New York City.

While the Queen City can't offer authentic smoked barbeque or an urban experience approaching Manhattan's, nearly everyone who knows Fox-Solomon is determined to entice her into staying and making a difference. Hers is just one of thousands of similar battles, fought every year, to restore the region's vitality.

The prospects of keeping the highly-lauded law school graduate, however, look dim.

"Honestly, there's not much keeping her here except her boyfriend, and he's leaving pretty soon as well," said Sara Paulson, a friend of Fox-Solomon's. "She won't stay here just for the beef-on-wheck, that's for sure."

"A bright shining light"

Fox-Solomon came to UB as a freshman, after graduating near the top of her high school class in Delmar, near Albany. From the start, acquaintances say, she was determined to stand out in a class of nearly 3,000 freshman.

"She hung out and went out to all the usual spots like everybody else, but she took her classes more seriously than any of them," said Joann Crofts, Fox-Solomon's roommate for four years.

Though UB wasn't her top choice for college, she began to fit comfortably into the school's political science department. At the same time, she began to enjoy some of the local pleasures.

"She told me that she wouldn't be my friend anymore if I missed the Taste of Buffalo last summer," said friend Rachel Fine. "It's a good thing there's a lot of good food out there, I guess."

Within two years, Fox-Solomon was a managing editor at the campus newspaper, and already attracting attention at UB, even at Capen's fifth-floor offices.

"Liz was a bright shining light here, and I'd be heartbroken to see her go," said former UB President William R. Greiner. "If we can't find something to keep people like Elizabeth here in Buffalo, then we're doing something wrong."

Above all, social justice

After graduating at the top of her political science class, Fox-Solomon entered UB's law school, seeking to earn her law degree and pursue a career in public policy and defense. Volunteering with the city's domestic violence legal aid program, she has begun carving out a meaningful life's work, classmates say.

"She doesn't want to make a lot of money, she doesn't want to just become a well-published professor, she wants to help those less fortunate," said classmate Tara Midlik.

"She's really obsessed with just two words: social justice."

Law students interested in pursuing public work -- and, even more, making it their life's career -- are rare and getting rarer, said Jonathan Lipman, a partner at the Hodgson & Russ law firm.

"I can almost guarantee you, people like (Fox-Solomon) will be a highly-sought commodity by almost any city. Especially in a city like ours, we should be doing everything we can to keep her here," said Lipman.

Holding on

But as much as Fox-Solomon may enjoy her friends and summers in Buffalo, not even the offer of the area's top internship could keep her in Buffalo. After declining a position in Buffalo's federal court system, Fox-Solomon took an offer with the ACLU in Atlanta.

"I've spent five years in Buffalo, and I might spend seven, it looks like," said Fox-Solomon, who granted a brief interview to the News, saying she was "about to suffocate in an avalanche of RICO briefs."

"And if your stupid paper had given my boyfriend a job, after he worked there with no insurance for almost a year, maybe I'd stay a bit longer, anyways," she added.

Richard Stalricht, director of Keep Buffalo Young, a local organization seeking to increase the region's appeal to young adults, said that even if decent jobs were available for Fox-Solomon and her boyfriend, the "cloud of doom and gloom" over Buffalo is particularly unappealing.

"We've got a city with a control board that might any day now merge into some colossal city-county-tax-behemoth, the Sabres and Bills are at their near-worst right now, the population is aging and Main Street is as empty as ever," said Stalricht. "Would you stick around, waiting for everything to be fixed?"

Save the residents, save the city

Still, hope remains that students like Fox-Solomon can find a comfortable niche in Western New York.

"If she's looking for causes to get behind, we've got 'em by the barrel-full," said Linda Carpacci, assistant director of the Western New York Coalition for Peace. "There's work to be done in this city, lots of it. And her voice is one we need."

David Meehan, a friend of both Fox-Solomon and her boyfriend, whose name the News could not obtain(remember) said that with housing at record low prices in even the city's most gentrified areas, there will always be hope that Fox-Solomon and other young professionals will take a chance on the region.

"Who knows, maybe she'll just get too hungry living somewhere else and move back," said Meehan. "I mean, seriously, she can't go a month without sushi, or a week without a good hamburger."
